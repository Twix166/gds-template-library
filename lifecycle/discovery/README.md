# Discovery

Discovery helps a team understand whether there is a problem worth solving, who is affected, what constraints exist, and whether it is sensible to move into Alpha.

Do not use Discovery to design or build the final service. Use it to build enough evidence to make a responsible decision about what should happen next.

## Discovery outcomes

By the end of Discovery, the team should be able to explain:

- who the users are and what they are trying to do
- what the current problem is, including wider service context
- how the current journey works across channels, teams and systems
- which constraints are hard constraints and which could be changed
- what assumptions still need testing
- what options exist, including stopping or improving an existing service
- whether there is a viable, cost-effective Alpha to run
- what evidence supports the recommendation

## Suggested sequence

1. Start with the problem statement and stakeholder map.
2. Plan research around users, current journeys, service context and constraints.
3. Capture user needs, journey evidence and current-state blueprint evidence as research progresses.
4. Keep an assumptions log from the start and review it weekly.
5. Use the findings to compare options, including non-build options and stopping.
6. Create a findings report and evidence pack only when there is enough evidence to support a decision.

See [Discovery evidence flow](evidence-flow.md) for how the artefacts connect.

## Source guidance

Discovery templates reference public guidance and examples where available. Sources should be credited in each template under `Reference examples and sources`.

Where content comes from GOV.UK, GOV.UK blogs or other public sector sources, check the applicable licence before reusing or adapting it. Most GOV.UK content is available under the Open Government Licence v3.0 unless otherwise stated.

## Completion checklist

Before marking a Discovery template as `complete`, check that it:

- gives teams a concrete artefact structure, not just high-level advice
- includes fill-in tables or prompts that can be copied into a working document
- links to at least one worked example
- credits public guidance or known good examples where relevant
- separates evidence from assumptions and recommendations
- avoids implying that the format is mandatory GDS guidance
